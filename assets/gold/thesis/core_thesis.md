# Core gold thesis

Gold is not driven by one variable. It sits at the intersection of rates, currency, inflation, liquidity, and geopolitical stress.

## Primary drivers
1. Real yields
2. US dollar direction
3. Geopolitical stress
4. Central bank demand
5. Growth and recession expectations

## Secondary transmission channels
1. Oil and broader energy prices
2. Inflation shock vs growth shock
3. Safe-haven demand vs forced liquidation
4. Financial conditions and liquidity stress
5. Commodity-complex spillovers

## Interpretation framework

### 1. Real yields
- Falling real yields usually support gold.
- Rising real yields usually pressure gold.
- Real yields are often the cleanest macro signal for gold.

### 2. US dollar
- Dollar weakness usually supports gold.
- Dollar strength usually pressures gold.
- In crisis periods, gold and the dollar can rise together if global fear is high.

### 3. Geopolitics
- War, sanctions, shipping disruption, and regional instability can support gold.
- But geopolitical stress does not automatically mean bullish gold.
- If the same shock also pushes real yields and the dollar higher, gold can struggle or become choppy.

### 4. Oil and energy
- Oil matters because it can change inflation expectations, growth expectations, and rate expectations.
- A rising oil shock can support gold if markets focus on inflation risk and instability.
- A rising oil shock can hurt gold if it pushes yields and the dollar sharply higher.
- Energy shocks are therefore a transmission channel, not a simple bullish input by themselves.

### 5. Inflation vs growth regime
- Gold tends to do better when inflation risk rises but policy is expected to ease or real yields fall.
- Gold tends to do worse when inflation risk rises and markets price tighter-for-longer policy.
- In a recession scare, gold can benefit if yields fall.
- In a liquidation event, gold can sell off temporarily even if the medium-term thesis is supportive.

### 6. Demand support
- Central bank buying and ETF inflows can reinforce bullish conditions.
- These are supportive forces, but they do not always override restrictive macro.

## Practical decision logic
When evaluating gold, always ask:

1. Are real yields helping or hurting?
2. Is the dollar helping or hurting?
3. Is geopolitical stress creating safe-haven demand?
4. Is oil/energy creating an inflation shock, a growth shock, or both?
5. Is gold actually behaving like a haven, or is macro pressure dominating?
6. Are central-bank and ETF flows reinforcing the move?

## Bottom line
Gold is strongest when:
- real yields are falling,
- the dollar is flat or weakening,
- geopolitical stress is elevated,
- and demand support is present.

Gold is weakest when:
- real yields are rising,
- the dollar is strengthening,
- and inflation or war shocks are causing tighter-for-longer expectations rather than easier policy.
