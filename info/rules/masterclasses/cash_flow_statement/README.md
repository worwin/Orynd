# Cash Flow Statement Masterclass

Scope: Chapters `C50` through `C52`

## Goal
Judge whether reported earnings become real cash and how much of that cash is left after reinvestment.

## Required Output
- `cash_flow_rating`: `Strong`, `Mixed`, or `Weak`
- `operating_cash_flow_view`
- `capex_burden_view`
- `buyback_quality_view`
- `cash_conversion_notes`
- `financing_dependence_view`

## Metric Rules
- Operating cash flow should support the income statement. The cash flow statement is the reality check on accrual accounting.
  Source: `C50`
- Capital expenditures should be judged over a long run, not in one isolated year.
  Positive pattern: capex uses `50% or less` of long-run net earnings.
  Especially strong pattern: capex uses `less than 25%` of long-run net earnings.
  Source: `C51`
- Buybacks are positive when they are funded by surplus cash flow.
  They are weaker when they are funded by debt or when they merely hide weak business economics.
  Source: `C52`
- Financing inflows should not be doing the work that operations should be doing.
  Source: `C50`, `C52`

## Hard Negatives
- Weak operating cash flow relative to reported profits.
- Capex that consumes most or all of earnings over time.
- Debt-funded buybacks.
- Financing inflows masking weak operations.

## Rating Guide
- `Strong`: good operating cash flow, low capex burden, and buybacks funded by excess cash.
- `Mixed`: acceptable cash conversion, but capex or financing quality is not ideal.
- `Weak`: poor cash conversion, capex-heavy economics, or debt-funded buyback optics.
