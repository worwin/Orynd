# Balance Sheet Masterclass

Scope: Chapters `C21` through `C49`

## Goal
Judge resilience, leverage, capital structure quality, and whether the business is truly self-financing.

## Required Output
- `balance_sheet_rating`: `Strong`, `Mixed`, or `Weak`
- `cash_strength_view`
- `inventory_and_receivables_view`
- `current_ratio_view`
- `asset_intensity_view`
- `short_term_debt_view`
- `long_term_debt_view`
- `adjusted_debt_to_equity_view`
- `retained_earnings_view`
- `treasury_stock_view`
- `roe_quality_view`
- `special_cases_and_exceptions`

## Metric Rules
- Cash and marketable securities are one of Buffett's first balance-sheet checks.
  Positive pattern: lots of cash, little or no debt, no obvious reliance on new share issuance or asset sales, plus consistent earnings.
  Lookback: review the past `7 years` of balance sheets to see what created the cash hoard.
  Source: `C24`
- Inventory matters mainly for manufacturers.
  Positive pattern: inventory and net earnings rise together.
  Negative pattern: inventory ramps up and then collapses, which often signals boom-bust economics.
  Source: `C25`
- Receivables should grow rationally with the business and should not obviously outrun sales quality.
  Source: `C26`
- Prepaids and other current assets are low-priority context items, not primary moat detectors.
  Source: `C27`
- Current ratio is contextual, not decisive, in this framework.
  Strong businesses can run below `1.0` because earning power covers current liabilities.
  Do not auto-fail a company for a sub-`1.0` current ratio if the earnings engine is strong.
  Source: `C28`, `C39`
- Lower dependence on PP&E is generally better because it implies lower capital intensity.
  Source: `C29`
- Goodwill mainly shows that acquisitions were made above book value. It is an acquisition clue, not a moat test by itself.
  Source: `C30`
- Reported intangible assets are not a reliable moat detector by themselves.
  Internally developed brands and franchises may not appear on the balance sheet at all.
  For brand businesses, rely more on long-run earnings power than on the intangible-asset line.
  Source: `C31`
- Long-term investments can be positive if they reflect disciplined deployment of excess cash from a strong core business.
  Source: `C32`
- Other long-term assets are low-priority context items.
  Source: `C33`
- Return on total assets is a useful efficiency check, but this section does not give a hard Buffett threshold for it.
  Source: `C34`
- Prefer little or no short-term debt.
  This is especially important for financial institutions that can be hurt by rollover risk.
  Source: `C37`
- Long-term debt should be modest.
  Buffett's historic purchases suggest the business should be able to repay its long-term debt within about `3 to 4 years` of earnings or less.
  Source: `C40`
- Treasury-share-adjusted debt-to-equity is the better leverage test for buyback-heavy businesses.
  Outside financial institutions, `below 0.80` is a positive sign.
  Always adjust for treasury stock before reading this ratio.
  Source: `C42`
- Preferred stock is usually a negative sign in this framework because wonderful businesses often do not need expensive preferred financing.
  Its absence is a positive signal.
  Source: `C44`
- Retained earnings should generally grow over time because productive retention supports compounding.
  Source: `C45`
- Treasury stock and a repurchase history are positive when they come from strong economics rather than balance-sheet strain.
  Source: `C46`
- ROE is important, but it must be interpreted carefully.
  `20% to 30%+` sustained ROE is strong when not mainly caused by leverage or buybacks.
  Negative equity can occur in exceptional buyback-heavy businesses, so do not treat it as an automatic failure.
  Source: `C47`, `C48`
- High ROE created mainly by leverage is a false positive.
  Source: `C49`

## Special Cases
- Financial institutions need their own context.
  Debt and liability ratios naturally run much higher there than in ordinary operating companies.
  Source: `C42`, `C37`
- Negative shareholders' equity can mean two very different things:
  strong buyback economics or real weakness.
  You must distinguish those cases before rating the company.
  Source: `C42`, `C48`
- Low reported intangible assets do not mean the business lacks a durable brand moat.
  Source: `C31`

## Hard Negatives
- Weak cash position plus heavy debt.
- Short-term debt dependence.
- Long-term debt that would take many years of earnings to repay.
- Adjusted debt-to-equity well above `0.80` outside financials.
- Preferred-stock dependence.
- Negative equity caused by weakness rather than strong buybacks.
- High ROE that is mostly leverage-driven.

## Rating Guide
- `Strong`: operating cash strength, conservative debt, good capital structure, rising retained earnings, and high-quality ROE.
- `Mixed`: serviceable balance sheet, but leverage, capital intensity, or special-case accounting still needs caution.
- `Weak`: refinancing risk, leverage-heavy economics, weak cash position, or poor capital-structure quality.
