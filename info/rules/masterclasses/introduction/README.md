# Introduction Masterclass

Scope: Chapters `C01` through `C06`

## Goal
Decide whether the company deserves a full Buffett-style review at all.
This section is a gatekeeper, not a valuation engine.

## Required Output
- `candidate_for_full_review`: `yes` or `no`
- `candidate_rating`: `Strong`, `Mixed`, or `Weak`
- `moat_hypothesis`
- `business_model_type`
- `durability_evidence`
- `rejection_reasons`

## Rule Set
- Durable competitive advantage must be the first question.
  Source: `C01`, `C02`
- Price does not rescue a weak business. The business-quality question comes before the valuation question.
  Source: `C01`, `C02`
- Prefer one of three business-model types:
  `unique product`, `unique service`, or `low-cost operator`.
  Source: `C03`
- Prefer institution-level economics rather than people-specific economics.
  Source: `C03`
- Prefer businesses whose product or service can remain relevant for a long time without constant redesign.
  Source: `C04`
- Prefer consistency in financial evidence across many years, not just one strong period.
  Source: `C04`, `C05`, `C06`
- Use primary filings and long statement histories as the working memory.
  Source: `C06`

## Hard Stops
- No believable moat hypothesis.
- Business model is too fast-changing or too hard to understand.
- Economics depend on short-term trading behavior rather than business quality.
- Business is people-dependent rather than institution-dependent.
- Filing history is too thin to judge durability.

## Rating Guide
- `Strong`: clear moat hypothesis, understandable business, durable demand, and enough statement history to continue.
- `Mixed`: some moat evidence exists, but durability or business-model clarity is still uncertain.
- `Weak`: no durable-advantage case or the business does not fit the Buffett framework.
